§1 The Sequencing Problem Core Argument
In commissioned BCW-based design — where budget and target population are defined before work begins — cost considerations appear at the APEASE stage, after diagnostic investment. This creates a structural gap: design work may produce options that are behaviourally sound but financially incompatible.
Gap 1 — Sequencing
Cost Too Late
Cost-feasibility analysis appears at APEASE (late), not before diagnosis (early). In commissioned programmes, budget is known from day one — making this a structural inefficiency.
Gap 2 — Correction Locus
Where to Correct?
BCW does not require the designer to determine where correction should occur before selecting an intervention. Subject, intervention, system, and economic failure are conflated.
Gap 3 — Mechanism Transfer
Activation ≠ Transfer
A mechanism activated inside a programme may not transfer to the real-world target context. Distinct from efficacy — the intervention can work internally and still fail externally.

§2 Cost Per Target Person — Benchmark Ranges Table 1 · Design Filter
CTP = Total full-loaded budget ÷ Planned target population. Benchmarked against published unit costs from comparable programmes. The benchmark ratio converts an abstract CTP into a decision signal. All values are indicative design-stage orientations only.

§4 / §7 Mechanism Transfer — Shepard-Type Analogue Transfer Assessment
Activation is not transfer. A mechanism activated within a facilitated programme setting may fail to transfer because contextual cues, social reinforcement, or resources present during the programme are absent in the real-world target context. Contextual distance predicts transfer loss.
Transfer Assessment — 3 Design Questions
  • Q1 — Contextual featuresWhat cues, supports, social structures, and environmental affordances enable the mechanism to operate within the intervention context?
  • Q2 — Feature presenceWhich of these features are present in the target-use environment, and which are absent?
  • Q3 — Bridge or redesign?For each absent feature: is there a design element that can bridge the gap, or does the mechanism require redesign to operate without it?
Shepard (1987): probability of generalisation decays as a function of distance in psychological space. Used here as a structural analogue — not a direct mathematical application.

§5 / §8 Design Feasibility Ratio — QALY Ceiling Macro-Economic Gate
QALY-based cost-effectiveness frameworks are methodologically rigorous but inaccessible at design stage. The DFR translates NICE threshold logic into a calculable design-stage ceiling — not replacing full economic evaluation, but imposing economic discipline before budget is committed.
Max acceptable CTP = Expected QALY gain per person × Cost-effectiveness threshold DFR = Proposed CTP / Maximum acceptable CTP DFR < 1.0 → Economically admissible DFR ≈ 1.0 → Borderline — requires strong justification DFR > 1.0 → Economically implausible — restructure or demonstrate additional value
DFR < 1.0
Economically admissible ✓
Proceed subject to benchmark ratio and practicability checks. DFR < 0.1 signals substantial headroom for additional investment if the behavioural case warrants it.
DFR ≈ 1.0
Borderline ⚠
Requires strong justification, sensitivity analysis, and risk disclosure. Re-examine QALY estimate and delivery assumptions before proceeding.
DFR > 1.0
Economically implausible ✕
Restructure design, reduce scope, or demonstrate additional value not captured in QALY estimate. Do not proceed without revision.

DREFS maps to Gap 2 (Intervention Design) specifically — Dose, Reach, Engagement, Fidelity, and Schedule represent the five dimensions along which an intervention can fail to produce its intended effect even when the BCT is theoretically appropriate.

§9 / §10 Implications for Design Practice 4 Actions
If the argument is accepted, four practical implications follow for any BCW-based design process.
Implication 1
Cost-Feasibility First Step
Any BCW-based design process should begin with a cost-feasibility step: calculate CTP, compare to benchmark ranges, derive the benchmark ratio, and constrain the function search space before COM-B/TDF diagnosis begins. This adds one step at the start and removes potentially many at APEASE.
Implication 2
Explicit Gate in BCW Guidance
Any BCW-based design framework — published guide, software tool, or internal methodology — should incorporate an explicit cost-feasibility gate at the opening of the design sequence. The BCW book does not currently specify this. Adding it would align BCW practice with established cost analysis methodology.
Implication 3
DFR as Standard Check
The DFR should be calculated as a standard check whenever QALY estimates are available for the target behaviour. It requires only a literature-based QALY estimate and the current threshold. Both are accessible at design stage for most established behaviour change targets.
Implication 4
Treat CTP as Diagnostic Tool
A benchmark ratio above 1.3× is not a reason to abandon a design — it is a prompt to examine why the unit cost is high and whether the expected value justifies it. A DFR below 0.1 signals substantial headroom for additional investment if the behavioural case warrants it.
